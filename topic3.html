Topic 3

Onion Services, Dual-Use Impact, and What's Next

Beyond anonymous browsing of the public internet, Tor supports a fully self-contained ecosystem of hidden servers, and its anonymity guarantees are used by very different people for very different reasons.

How onion services work

An onion service lets an operator host a website (or other service) without ever revealing its server's location, reachable only through a unique, cryptographically derived address. Connecting to one skips the cleartext exit nodes entirely:

  1. The service builds circuits to several relays called introduction points and publishes its address to a distributed directory.
  2. A client picks a random relay to act as a rendezvous point and builds a circuit to it.
  3. The client sends an encrypted introduction message, through an introduction point, naming the rendezvous point and a one-time authorization token.
  4. The onion service builds its own circuit to that rendezvous point, matches the token, and the two three-hop circuits are joined.

The result is a six-hop connection where neither side learns the other's real network location.

A dual-use technology

That same anonymity guarantee cuts both ways:

  • Protective uses: investigative journalists, whistleblowers, and human rights defenders in authoritarian regions rely on onion services to transmit leaks about corruption or abuses without exposing their location.
  • Malicious uses: the same guarantees are exploited by dark-web marketplaces, ransomware operators, and state-sponsored threat groups, whose infrastructure resists domain seizure because it lives on onion services rather than the regular DNS system.

Why security teams use Tor too

For cybersecurity and OSINT analysts, visiting a threat actor's command-and-control infrastructure directly from a corporate network is dangerous - it exposes the organization and tips off the adversary that it's under investigation. Tor gives analysts managed attribution: probes and monitoring appear to come from shifting, random exit nodes instead of the analyst's real network.

Where Tor is still vulnerable

Tor's low-latency design (it doesn't intentionally delay or reorder packets) makes it vulnerable to traffic correlation: an adversary who can watch both the entry and exit traffic at once may match the timing and volume of a data spike entering a guard node to an identical spike leaving an exit node, breaking the anonymity of that circuit. Most ISPs only see one end of that equation - but large, cross-jurisdictional network operators may not.

The quantum computing question

Tor's circuit handshakes currently rely on elliptic-curve Diffie-Hellman, which a sufficiently powerful quantum computer running Shor's algorithm could break. Adversaries are already running "store now, decrypt later" strategies, archiving encrypted Tor traffic today in hopes of reading it once quantum hardware catches up. In response, the Tor Project is prototyping hybrid handshakes that combine traditional elliptic-curve cryptography with post-quantum key-encapsulation mechanisms.

Takeaway: Tor's biggest engineering challenge isn't the math inside a single connection - it's staying ahead of adversaries who can watch the network from the outside, whether that's through timing attacks today or quantum cryptanalysis tomorrow.
