Origins and Evolution of Tor

Tor did not start out as a tool for the general public. Its foundational ideas were built to solve a very specific government intelligence problem, and it only became useful for that purpose once it was opened up to everyone else.

A U.S. Naval Research Laboratory project

The concepts behind onion routing were developed in the mid-1990s by mathematicians and computer scientists at the United States Naval Research Laboratory: Paul Syverson, Michael Reed, and David Goldschlag. Their original goal was not to protect illegal activity, but to secure government intelligence communications and operational security for personnel working in foreign environments.

Why a private network wasn't the answer

It might seem like the obvious solution would be a dedicated, private government network. But that approach has a flaw: if intelligence personnel used a network that only government traffic touched, any adversary watching that network could immediately tell the traffic was American in origin, simply because nothing else looked like it. A public network, mixing civilian, corporate, and government users together, was mathematically necessary to create real "hidden in plain sight" anonymity, the bigger and more diverse the crowd, the harder any one person is to pick out of it.

  • Goal: protect the identity of the sender, not just the content of a message
  • Problem: a private/dedicated network reveals who's using it just by existing
  • Solution: build a network diverse enough that traffic blends into the crowd

From a lab project to a public non-profit

In 2002, this early research evolved into the third-generation onion routing project, led by Roger Dingledine and Nick Mathewson. They recognized that strong anonymity depends on a large, decentralized user base - the bigger the crowd, the better the cover. So the Naval Research Laboratory released the underlying source code under a free and open-source license, inviting the public to build and use it.

By 2006, the Tor Project was established as a Massachusetts-based research and educational non-profit responsible for maintaining the software and scaling the network. Today, Tor no longer depends on government-run servers at all. It runs entirely on thousands of volunteer-operated relays spread across jurisdictions all over the world.

Why this history matters: Tor's anonymity guarantees only work because ordinary people, companies, and researchers all share the same network. That's a direct engineering consequence of the "hidden in plain sight" problem described above, not an accident of how the project grew.
